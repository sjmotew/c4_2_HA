# Is this for you?

You've seen the [case for leaving](why-leave-control4.md) and [what it costs](what-youre-signing-up-for.md). This is the gut check. Five minutes, honest answers, before you spend a dollar or pull a zone.

There's no passing score that obligates you to migrate. The point is to surface the one or two answers that should give you pause — so you go in clear-eyed, or decide it's not your project, before it's expensive to learn that.

## The self-assessment

Answer each honestly. Not "could I, in theory" — "will I, in practice."

### 1. Home networking comfort

**The floor you need:** you can log into your router, find a device's IP address, and you don't panic when something needs a static address or a port. You don't need to subnet a data center.

- *Comfortable* → you're ready; the rest you'll pick up.
- *Shaky but willing* → fine, but budget time to shore this up first; it's the foundation everything sits on.
- *This paragraph stressed me out* → this is the one prerequisite that's hard to migrate without. Address it before anything else, or reconsider.

### 2. Tolerance for breakage

A zone mid-migration will occasionally misbehave. That's expected — it's *why* the old system stays live as a fallback. But "occasionally broken for an evening" has to be survivable for you and the household.

- *I can live with a zone being flaky while I sort it* → good; that's the normal texture of the work.
- *Any downtime would be a crisis* → either your tolerance or your household's is too tight for comfort; lean harder on parallel operation and slow down, or this may not be for you.

### 3. The household's tolerance

This is a separate question from your own, and it's the one people skip. The people you live with did not sign up for a project.

- *They're patient, or won't notice if I sequence it well* → you have the room to do this right.
- *They will absolutely notice and absolutely mind* → not a stop sign, but it raises the bar: zero disruption goes from a principle to an ironclad rule, and your pace slows accordingly.

### 4. Time budget

This is months of evenings, not a weekend. Be honest about what you actually have, not what you wish you had.

- *I have steady, recurring time* → ideal; steady beats heroic.
- *Only sporadic bursts* → workable if you migrate in small, self-contained zones that survive being left alone between sessions. Avoid leaving a zone half-done.
- *Almost none* → wait until you do. A migration started and abandoned mid-stream is worse than not starting.

### 5. Willingness to document as you go

The quiet predictor. The people who [write it down as they go](../framework/principles.md) finish; the people who plan to "remember it" stall when their own system becomes a mystery to them.

- *I'll keep notes* → strongest single signal you'll see this through.
- *I'll remember* → you won't. If you're unwilling to document, weight that heavily.

## Reading your answers

There's no points total. Instead, look for the shape:

- **Mostly "comfortable / willing," with notes and time?** You're a strong fit. The rest of this guide is built for you — start with the [framework](../framework/).
- **One weak spot — networking, time, or documentation?** Fixable. Shore up that single thing *before* you start rather than discovering it under pressure mid-migration.
- **Two or more in the "this stressed me out" column?** Be honest with yourself. That's not a skill gap you'll casually close mid-project; it's a sign the timing or the fit is wrong. There is no shame in staying — the worst migration is the one taken on by someone who wanted the result but not the work.

The deciding factor was never talent. It's appetite for ownership plus the honesty to prepare. If both are there, read on.
